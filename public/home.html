Project Info

About the Project

Between the Lines is a creative intervention responding to the lived experiences of international students who have been impacted by the influence of the UK border control system on Higher Education Institutions. The project uses DNA data storage technology to embed stories from the community back into the administrative systems that govern them.

DNA data storage is an emerging technology that allows digital information to be encoded into laboratory created DNA. Much like we can store data on memory sticks or the cloud, DNA can also be used as a digital storage medium. The resulting synthetic DNA can be preserved for thousands of years, and the digital data can be retrieved via DNA sequencing.

In this instance, the synthetic DNA has been embedded into writing ink and contains the written testimony of students who have faced difficult immigration practices, even from inside the UK higher education institutions in which they are studying.

1. Storing the data

To create Between the Lines, stories and experiences from the international student community were collected and compiled into a text document. The text document was then digitised and encoded into DNA nucleotide sequences by Twist Bioscience. Using this sequence, scientists at Twist then constructed synthetic DNA according to the nucleotide sequence.

The resulting synthetic DNA was delivered in small, airtight metal capsules (see image, capsule photographed next to a one penny coin for scale). Each capsule contains close to 200,000 copies of the original text document stored in dried form.

What is DNA Data Storage?

Much data can be stored on memory sticks or the cloud, DNA can also be used to store digital information. DNA data storage is an emerging technology that allows digital information to be encoded into laboratory created DNA.

Computers process and store digital information using binary code, a language consisting of only 0’s and 1’s. Information stored in DNA works in a similar way. DNA is made up of four chemical building blocks, or ‘DNA nucleotides’. These are: adenine (A), guanine (G), cytosine (C) and thymine (T). The sequence of these blocks determine the biological instructions that they carry. For example, ATCGTT might instruct for blue eyes, while ATCGCT might instruct for brown.

DNA data storage involves converting the binary code of a digital file into DNA nucleotide sequences on a computer.

The Intervention

Between the Lines infiltrates the administrative systems of Higher Education institutions throughout the UK.

The pens, along with the lived experiences they hold embedded within the ink, have been posted out to the administration hubs of all HE Student Sponsors.

Along with the pens, the recipient receives a letter inviting them to be the new keeper of the pen - should they choose to accept the responsibility it carries.

The new pen keepers are instructed to use the pens when undertaking administrative tasks on behalf of their institution, so that the lived experiences of those impacted can permeate the administrative systems that govern them.

As the keepers use the pens, the stories within the ink transfer to the paper - embedding the lived experiences of the international students back into the administrative systems that govern them.

The Process

Stories and experiences from international students were collected and compiled into a text document.

The text document was converted into binary data (1's and 0's).

The document was sent to Twist Bioscience, who's Scientists encoded the binary data into DNA nucleotides and synthesised DNA strands according the the sequence produced.

The resulting synthetic DNA was sent back in dried form inside a small, sealed metal capsule.

The dried DNA was extracted from the metal capsules and added to writing ink. The ink was then injected into pens.

The pens were posted to every Higher Education provider who is a registered Student Sponser. A full list of registered student sponsers can be found on the UK government website here .
